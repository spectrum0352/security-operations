## Threat Modelling 

- It helps you identify potential threats and risk reduction strategies earlier in the development lifecycle.

- Threat modeling uses a data-flow diagram that graphically shows how system works. It then applies a framework to help you find and fix security issues.

- Determining Potential Attacks: Data Flow Diagrams - Entities, Technologies, Transactions, Attacks vs each element.

- Reduction Analysis

  - Decomposing system/process/environment: Modules, Functions, Protocols, etc.

  - Identify the Following: Trust Boundaries, Data Flow Paths, Input Points, Privileged Operations, Security Approach

- Prioritization and Response

- Probability x Damage Potential

- High/Medium/Low

- **When to use threat modeling?** Use threat modeling whenever you design new systems or update existing ones.

- **Who can threat model?** Anyone with a working knowledge of the system and a basic understanding of security can work with threat modeling.

- **Threat modelling Process**

  - Decompose the application or infrastructure

  - Determine the threats

  - Determine countermeasures and mitigations

  - Rank the threats

  - **Design**: Capture all requirements for your system and create a data-flow diagram.

  - **Break**: Apply threat modelling framework to data-flow diagram and find potential security issues.

  - **Fix**: Decide how to approach each issue with the right combination of security controls.

  - **Verify**: Verify requirements are met, issues are found, and security controls are implemented.

- **Purpose of threat modelling:** Purpose of threat modelling could be answered by below questions:

  - What are we working on?

  - What can go wrong?

  - What are we going to do about it?

  - Did we do a good job?

- **Frameworks of threat modelling**

  - Structured approaches for mapping threat called as frameworks or methodologies

  - **STRIDE (by Microsoft)**

> Each category of STRIDE violates a specific property from variations of the CIA triad:

- **Spoofing**: Impersonating another person or computer to gain access, which violates <u>authenticity</u>.

- **Tampering**: Unauthorized changes in data, code, configuration, which violates <u>integrity</u>.

- **Repudiation:** It means making it impossible to link an action you performed to you, which violates <u>non-repudiability.</u> Denying having done an action

- **Information disclosure:** Revelation of controlled information to unauthorized person or entity, which violates <u>confidentiality</u>.

- **Denial of service:** Prevents the use of an asset, which violates <u>availability</u>.

- **Elevation of privilege:** Gain capabilities without proper authorization. which violates <u>authorization</u>.

- **Threat Modelling Best Practices**

  - Make threat modelling a priority during system development.

  - Do not to view applications and systems in isolation from one another.

  - This result is a comprehensive attack surface which the CISO can use to understand the entire threat portfolio across the enterprise

  - Don't focus too much on whatever threats are in the headlines

  - Don't forget that your users can be some of the biggest inadvertent threats of all.

  - Don't forget that a threat model should be a living document and needs constant updating.

- **Threat Modelling Approaches**

  - Threat Identification approaches:

    - Focused on Assets: **Protect valuable assets**

    - Focused on Attackers: **Protect the things that attackers want to attack**

    - Focused on Software: **Protect the software**

    - Individual Threats: **Be cautious of Contractors and Trusted Partners**

  - **Proactive**

    - Performed before and while the system is being implemented

    - Predicting threats and designing defences in advance

    - More cost effective and more successful

    - Security Development Lifecycle

      - Reduce number of coding defects

      - Reduce severity of remaining defects

  - **Reactive**

    - Performed after the system has been implemented

    - Less effective but more cost effective than redesign

    - E.g., penetration testing, source code review, fuzz testing

    - Fuzz Testing: Random invalid input is fed to a program. Attempts to find previously undetected flaws

# Data Security: A Balancing Act

- **Permanent Data Security:** Unfortunately, completely eliminating the risk of data breaches is impossible. However, you can significantly reduce the risk by:

  - Defining clear procedures for data cleanup and disposal.

- **Vulnerability Severity:**

  - **Critical Vulnerability (CVSS v3 Base Score 9-10):** These vulnerabilities can be easily exploited and have a severe impact. They require immediate attention.

  - **High Vulnerability (CVSS v3 Base Score 7-8.9):** These are still significant vulnerabilities that should be addressed promptly.

- **Vulnerability Scans:** Automated searches for known weaknesses in systems and software.

- **Bug Bounties:** Companies offer rewards to ethical hackers (bug bounty hunters) to find vulnerabilities before malicious actors do. This provides a fresh perspective on potential security issues.

- **Common Security Vulnerabilities:**

  - Weak access controls (e.g., easy-to-guess passwords)

  - Cryptographic issues (e.g., weak encryption algorithms)

  - Injection flaws (e.g., SQL injection)

  - Insecure design flaws in software or systems

  - Misconfigured security settings

  - Outdated software with known vulnerabilities

  - Weak authentication processes

  - Data integrity issues (e.g., data easily altered)

  - Poor logging and monitoring practices

  - Server-side request forgery (SSRF) vulnerabilities

- **Unpatched Vulnerabilities:** There are several reasons why companies might have unpatched vulnerabilities:

  - **Resource constraints:** Fixing vulnerabilities can be time-consuming and expensive.

  - **Downtime concerns:** Patching can sometimes require system downtime, which can disrupt business operations.

  - **Lack of awareness:** Not all companies prioritize cybersecurity or may not be aware of all their vulnerabilities.

- **Vulnerability vs. Exploit:**

  - **Vulnerability:** A weakness in a system that can be exploited. (Think of it as a hole in your fence.)

  - **Exploit:** The act of taking advantage of a vulnerability to gain unauthorized access. (Think of someone climbing through the hole in your fence.)

**Threat Hunting: Benefits with Realistic Expectations**

While threat hunting offers significant advantages, it's crucial to communicate its limitations to clients:

- **Limited Scope:** **88% of clients** felt improvement was needed, indicating not all threats are guaranteed to be found.

- **Transparency Concerns:** **53% of respondents** worried about revealing too much hunting info, potentially benefiting attackers.

- **Time and Resource Intensive:** **56% of respondents** felt the process was cumbersome, highlighting the need for skilled personnel, advanced tools, and ongoing effort.

By acknowledging these drawbacks, you demonstrate a well-rounded understanding of threat hunting and its role within a broader security strategy.

**Why Internal Threats Are More Dangerous than external threats?**

Internal threats pose a greater risk than external threats for several reasons:

- **Familiarity Breeds Complacency:** People become accustomed to seeing someone around regularly, lowering their guard and potentially missing suspicious behavior.

- **Greater Access:** Insiders have legitimate access to systems and data, allowing them to bypass security measures and cause more damage.

**Examples of Insider Threats:**

- **Disgruntled Employee:** An unhappy employee might sell their credentials or sabotage systems before leaving the company.

- **Social Engineering:** Someone disguises themselves as a legitimate visitor (e.g., delivery person) to gain access to sensitive information.

**External threats** typically lack the same level of access and familiarity, making them easier to detect and prevent.
